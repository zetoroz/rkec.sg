How to choose a renovation contractor in Singapore: 10 questions to ask

Anto Jose · Director, RK E&C · Updated 15 July 2026 · 9 min read

Choosing a renovation contractor in Singapore comes down to one thing: knowing the right questions to ask before you sign. Most renovation disputes don't start with bad workmanship — they start with a vague quote, a missing contract, or a deposit handed over to a firm nobody checked. The ten questions below are what we'd want a homeowner to ask any renovation contractor in Singapore, including us. Get clear answers to all of them and you've already filtered out most of the risk.

Why the questions matter more than the price

It's tempting to shortlist on price alone, line up three quotes and pick the lowest. The trouble is that renovation quotes are rarely comparing the same thing — different materials, different scope, different assumptions about what's "included". A low number often just means something has been left out, and that gap reappears later as a variation order. The questions that follow let you compare contractors on the things that actually predict a smooth job: registration, clarity, accountability and how they handle the inevitable changes.

Renovation is also a months-long relationship, not a one-off transaction. You'll be dealing with this firm through dust, delays and the small decisions that come up once walls are open. How clearly someone answers your questions at the quoting stage is a fair preview of how they'll communicate when a tile is out of stock or a measurement doesn't match the drawings. Treat the conversation itself as part of the evidence, not just the number at the bottom of the page.

The 10 questions to ask before you sign

Work through these with every contractor you're considering. Strong firms answer them plainly and in writing; the ones to avoid get vague or defensive.

  1. Are you a registered company, and are you on HDB's contractor directory? Ask for the UEN and confirm the firm is properly incorporated. For HDB flats, check it appears in HDB's Directory of Renovation Contractors — firms there are registered under HDB's Registered Renovation Contractors' Scheme and have agreed to follow HDB's renovation rules.
  2. Can you give me an itemised fixed-price quote? You want each item, quantity, material and price spelled out — not one lump sum. An itemised quote lets you compare like for like and see exactly what you're paying for.
  3. What's the payment schedule, and how much is the deposit? Look for staged payments tied to milestones, with the final balance due only after you've inspected and accepted the work. A demand for most of the money upfront is a warning sign.
  4. Who actually does the work — your own crew or sub-contractors? In-house crews give you a single point of accountability. If sub-contractors are used, ask who they are, who supervises them, and who you call when something goes wrong.
  5. What insurance do you carry? A responsible contractor holds public liability and work-injury coverage and arranges the renovation insurance HDB expects. Ask to see it before work starts.
  6. Will I get a written contract with a timeline? Everything agreed — scope, materials, prices, start and end dates — should be in a signed contract, not a chat thread. A timeline with milestones tells you whether the schedule is realistic.
  7. How are changes and variations handled? Agree that any change to scope is priced and approved in writing before it's carried out. This is the single biggest source of disputed final bills.
  8. Can I see your portfolio and speak to past clients? Photos show finish; references tell you how the contractor behaved when problems came up. Ask for recent jobs similar to yours.
  9. What happens if something goes wrong after handover? Ask about defects liability — how long after completion you can report issues, and how rectification is handled.
  10. Who is my point of contact during the works? Knowing the one person responsible for your project, and how to reach them, prevents the frustrating loop of being passed around mid-renovation.

The HDB Registered Renovation Contractors' Scheme

If you're renovating an HDB flat, this is the first filter. Contractors on HDB's Directory of Renovation Contractors are registered under the Registered Renovation Contractors' Scheme and have undertaken to follow HDB's renovation guidelines — the rules on permitted works, hacking, noise hours and which jobs need a permit.

Using a registered contractor doesn't guarantee a perfect job, but it does mean the firm is accountable to a scheme and familiar with what HDB allows. For works like hacking or demolition, that knowledge matters — see our guide on HDB hacking and demolition rules for what's permitted and what isn't.

Fixed-price itemised quote vs vague lump sum

This is where most cost surprises are born. A vague lump sum gives you one number with little breakdown — easy to under-quote, easy for scope to drift. An itemised fixed-price quote lists every item so you can interrogate it line by line and compare contractors fairly.

When you read a quote, check that material grades are specified (not just "tiles" but the size and type), that quantities are stated, and that anything excluded is named. If you're budgeting a flat, our breakdown of HDB renovation cost in Singapore shows where the money typically goes and what a realistic quote looks like.

Deposits and payment schedules

How a contractor structures payment tells you a lot. A healthy schedule ties money to progress: a modest deposit on signing, progress payments as key stages complete, and a final balance held back until you've inspected and accepted the work. That keeps the contractor motivated to finish well and protects you if things stall.

Be wary of anyone asking for the bulk of the contract value before meaningful work has begun. If a dispute does arise over payment or scope, CASE (the Consumers Association of Singapore) can advise on consumer contracts and help with disputes.

Who does the work, and how it's documented

Two contractors can quote the same job and run it completely differently. The questions of who holds the tools and what's written down are the ones homeowners most often skip — and they decide how accountable the job is.

  • In-house vs sub-con. An in-house crew means one company owns the outcome end to end. Sub-contracting isn't wrong, but you should know who's on site, who supervises, and who you call when an issue comes up.
  • The contract. A proper contract captures scope, materials, prices, timeline and defects liability. If it isn't written down, it isn't agreed — and it's hard to enforce later.
  • Variations in writing. Mid-job changes are normal. What protects you is the rule that every variation is quoted and signed off before work proceeds, so nothing surprises you on the final invoice.
