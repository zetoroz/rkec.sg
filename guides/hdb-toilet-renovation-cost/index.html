HDB toilet renovation cost: hacking, re-tiling & waterproofing

Hossain Md Iqbal · Project Lead, RK E&C · Updated 8 July 2026 · 8 min read

Planning to redo your HDB toilet? The short answer: an HDB toilet renovation cost in Singapore usually lands around $2,000 to $4,000+ all-in, depending on toilet size, whether you tile both floor and walls, your tile choice, and the sanitary ware and vanity you pick. A leaner job — hack the floor, waterproof, re-tile and reinstall the toilet bowl — can come in lower. Below is exactly what that money buys, the correct work sequence, and a real recent RK E&C job broken down line by line.

What an HDB toilet renovation actually involves

A proper toilet renovation is not just "new tiles". It is a sequence of steps that must be done in the right order, because each one relies on the one before it holding up:

  1. Hack the old tiles. The existing floor (and wall) tiles are chipped off down to the screed. This is dusty, noisy work and generates the bulk of the debris.
  2. Screed and prep. The exposed surface is cleaned, any damaged screed is patched, and falls are checked so water still runs to the floor trap.
  3. Waterproofing membrane. A waterproofing membrane is applied over the screed in coats and left to cure, then a water-ponding test confirms it holds before anything covers it.
  4. Lay the new tiles. New floor and wall tiles are set into adhesive, spaced and grouted once the waterproofing has cured.
  5. Reinstall the toilet bowl and fittings. The toilet bowl, basin, tap, shower set and accessories go back in, sealed and tested.

The one step you cannot skip is waterproofing. In an HDB block your toilet floor sits directly above someone else's ceiling. Miss or rush the membrane and water tracks through the slab and reappears as staining or dripping in the unit below — an inter-floor leak that is far more expensive, disruptive and neighbour-straining to fix than doing it right the first time. What drives the cost up or down

Two toilets of the same size can quote very differently. The main levers:

  • Toilet size. More floor and wall area means more tiles, more adhesive and more labour hours.
  • Wall + floor tiling vs floor only. Tiling the walls to full height roughly doubles the tiling scope. A floor-only refresh is the cheapest meaningful upgrade.
  • Tile choice. Standard ceramic is economical; large-format, porcelain or feature tiles cost more to buy and take more skill to lay flush.
  • Sanitary ware. A basic toilet bowl and mixer versus a rimless bowl, rain shower and designer basin is a real swing in the materials line.
  • Vanity and storage. A mirror cabinet or vanity unit adds carpentry and plumbing beyond the tiling.
  • Condition of the existing screed. Older flats sometimes need extra patching or re-screeding once the old tiles come off, which is only visible after hacking.

Signs your HDB toilet needs a re-do

A toilet renovation is not only cosmetic. Watch for these, because they point to a waterproofing failure that is quietly getting worse:

  • Damp patches or peeling paint on the ceiling below — the clearest sign the membrane has failed and water is passing through the slab.
  • Loose, hollow-sounding or cracked tiles — water may be sitting under them.
  • Persistent musty smell or mould in corners and grout lines that keeps returning after cleaning.
  • Water pooling and not draining toward the floor trap, hinting the falls are off.
  • Crumbling grout that lets water reach the screed.

If a neighbour below is complaining of a leak, treat it as urgent — a hack-and-rewaterproof is usually the durable fix, not just re-grouting.

The one-contractor advantage

You can hire separate people to hack, waterproof, tile and plumb — but it rarely goes well. The moment a leak shows up, each trade blames the next: the tiler says the waterproofing was bad, the waterproofer says the tiler punctured it. With one contractor doing hack + waterproof + tile + install, there is a single point of accountability from bare screed to a sealed, working toilet. It is also usually cheaper, because the work is scheduled back-to-back with one mobilisation rather than several separate trips.

How long it takes

Budget roughly 5 to 10 working days for a typical HDB toilet. The hacking is fast, but the schedule is set by curing, not demolition:

  • Hacking and debris removal — usually 1 day.
  • Screed prep and waterproofing coats plus curing and a ponding test — several days, and this cannot be rushed.
  • Tiling, then grout and adhesive setting — a few days.
  • Reinstalling sanitary ware and final sealing — around 1 day.

Any contractor promising a full hack-to-finish toilet in two days is almost certainly shortcutting the waterproofing cure — which is precisely the corner you do not want cut.

HDB rules to know

Toilet renovation is regulated because it affects the building fabric and your neighbours. A few practical points:

  • Renovation permit. Hacking tiles and related works generally require an HDB renovation permit, which a licensed contractor applies for on your behalf. See HDB's renovation guidelines for what needs approval.
  • Waterproofing rules. HDB sets conditions on how the original toilet waterproofing membrane may be treated, precisely to prevent inter-floor leaks.
  • Registered contractor. Use an HDB Registered Renovation Contractor so the permit, works and warranty are all in order.
  • Renovation hours. Noisy works like hacking must stay within HDB's permitted hours to keep the peace with neighbours.

For the broader construction and workmanship context, Singapore's Building and Construction Authority sets the standards good contractors build to.
